I found a go kart frame on Craigslist back in 2012 but never got around to putting anything in it. Daniel found that an old Geo Metro seat fit perfectly and we managed to source a motor, controller and contactor from a 6-person golf cart, ~48 volts, absolutely absurd for something this small. This should be fun, just have to learn how to weld and a bunch of other things.

## The Build

Daniel help with some of the welding and fabrication. We had to source an output and input gear from a farm supply shop, add the key, and try to weld some kind of real motor supports while failing to have enough scrap to tension the chain so that's the wood you see hanging off the back in the photos here.

![Oh is that wood? Random bolts and zip ties? You bet!](/images/blog/go-kart-roundup-2014/IMG_6248.jpg)

![Detail of contactor and primary control box for the motor.](/images/blog/go-kart-roundup-2014/IMG_6252.jpg)

The motor spline is a custom piece I designed and had 3D printed in stainless steel through ShapeWays. Getting a proper keyed output for gearing is one of those problems where you can either spend weeks machining something or spend an afternoon in CAD and send it to someone. Let's ignore the fact that this cost like $70, and I fucked up the first one and had to have another one made. Whoops! Still awesome.

![Nearly visible here, a custom designed 3D printed in stainless steel motor spline with a keyed output for gearing.](/images/blog/go-kart-roundup-2014/IMG_6255.jpg)

## First Movement

![Custom sheet metal dashboard made from an old desk](/images/blog/go-kart-roundup-2014/2014-04-23 19.34.07.jpg)

The dashboard is cut from an old metal desk. The contactor clicks, the controller hums, and then...

![Daniel gets the hot seat for trying to move under its own power, and yes it does rip a weld or two.](/images/blog/go-kart-roundup-2014/MVI_6256.mp4)

The torque from an 50V golf cart motor on a frame this light is genuinely terrifying and it broke our output gear welds without even trying. Progress! Proves the pedals, batteries, frame, seat etc. Still no differential so steering increases wheel slip and friction by like 2x, better be on dirt!

Sylvia gets some welding practice in on the motor supports. Don't be afraid to get your kids into electrical sunburn metal glue guns. Teaches tool respect if if they don't get a chance to do it again later in life.

The instant torque and quiet of electric powertrains is addictive. No clutch, no gear shifting, just push-and-go acceleration that presses you back in the seat. Now let's hope it doesn't rip itself to pieces before we can find out its range on 2 pairs of batteries we stole from an old server backup unit!
